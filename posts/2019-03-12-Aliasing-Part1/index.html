Aliasing is a phenomenon that shows up in a suprising number of ways in the world of digital audio and video. <motivational intro - why do I care?>

Like many emergent phenomena, there are a few different lenses through which we can think about it. In this series, we'll try to look at it and gain intuition from a few perspectives. I'll try to avoid mathematical terms and prerequisites as much as possible, although of course we'll learn mathematical ways of thinking throughout the series - that's kind of the point.

First, let's talk about movies. Let's imagine that we're making a movie of a spinning googly eye. It's gunna be a hit, I can feel it.

Movies are created by taking still pictures very rapidly - 24 times a second is the current standard. Each of these still pictures is a frame of the movie. Here's an illustration:

Googly eye spin rate: 0.05 cycles per second

Frame Rate: 2.00 frames per second

If this diagram seems overwhelming, don't worry, we'll break this down section by section.

On the left is the real googly eye we're taking a movie of. The googly eye is spinning at a rate of 0.05 cycles per second. You can adjust the rate by dragging the number left and right. In fact, any red number in this series can be adjusted this way.

In the center is the camera. This takes a photo of the googly eye 2.00 times in each second. This is represented by a timer bar - each time it fills up, we'll take a photo.

On the right is the movie we're recording. At high frame rates and low spin rates, this will look pretty close to the original googly eye, but at higher spin rates and lower frame rates, this will start to look pretty different.
